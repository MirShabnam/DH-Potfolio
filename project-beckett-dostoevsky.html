Two Faces
of the Void

Beckett's Waiting and Dostoevsky's Despair

A computational literary study of how Samuel Beckett and Fyodor Dostoevsky — writing from within radically different cultural and historical horizons — construct divergent literary philosophies of meaninglessness. One performs the void in the texture of the prose itself. The other contests it from within.

Research Question

Beckett writes after Auschwitz, after God, after language has failed. Dostoevsky writes before the Russian Revolution, in the shadow of Orthodox Christianity, still fighting against nihilism rather than embodying it. Both inhabit the void — but they inhabit it differently.

This project uses computational text analysis to map the formal and lexical dimensions of that difference, arguing that Beckett and Dostoevsky are not simply two writers about despair but two distinct cultural epistemologies of it.

Key Findings

"Beckett's language is characterised by extreme lexical reduction — short sentences, repeated negations, a vocabulary of absence. Dostoevsky's by contrast is expansive, contradictory, emotionally volatile. These formal differences are the philosophical argument."

The co-occurrence network reveals that Beckett's key terms cluster around: nothing, wait, cannot, again, silence, end, go. Dostoevsky's cluster around: soul, God, suffering, love, man, truth, reason, feel, live. Beckett's void is the void after all those words have become unusable. Dostoevsky's void is the space in which they are still being fought over.
