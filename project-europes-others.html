Colonial Power · Spatial History

Europe's Others

A Spatial History of Colonial Knowledge Extraction

A StoryMapJS spatial argument tracing ten moments where European institutions extracted knowledge, texts, specimens, and cultural objects from colonised territories — and incorporated them into European intellectual authority. The cumulative argument: European knowledge power was built on a global infrastructure of colonial theft.

Research Question

European intellectual authority — the authority of its universities, museums, scientific institutions, and publishing houses — was not built in isolation. It was built through systematic extraction: of plants, specimens, texts, artworks, linguistic knowledge, medical knowledge, and human beings from colonised territories across Asia, Africa, and the Americas.

This project makes that argument spatially. Ten map points. Ten stories. One cumulative argument about where European knowledge came from.

Key Finding

"Story Point 8 is the most personal. Kashmir's oldest literary texts — manuscripts in the Sharada script, the script of a tradition that produced Dr. Tarannum Riyaz's literary culture — are in the Bodleian Library in Oxford. This is not coincidence. It is the colonial archive, with direct consequences for what counts as Kashmiri cultural heritage today."

The spatial argument is cumulative. Any single point might be explained as an historical anomaly. Together, mapped across the globe, they constitute a pattern: European knowledge power was systematically constructed through the appropriation of non-European knowledge. The map is the argument.
