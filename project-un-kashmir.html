The Language
of Occupation

A Computational Analysis of UN Security Council Resolutions on Kashmir, 1948–2019

A Voyant Tools corpus analysis tracing how the international language of the Kashmir conflict transformed over seven decades — from the vocabulary of self-determination and democratic rights to the vocabulary of stability, bilateralism, and counterterrorism. The linguistic shift is itself a form of power.

Research Question

The UN Security Council passed its first resolution on Kashmir in January 1948 — calling for a ceasefire and, crucially, for a free plebiscite: a vote by the people of Jammu and Kashmir to determine their own political future. That promise has never been fulfilled.

What has happened instead is a slow transformation in the international language used to describe the conflict. This project tracks that transformation computationally, asking: what words disappear from the UN corpus over time, what words replace them, and what does that linguistic drift reveal about how institutional power shapes political possibility?

After 1971, formal UNSC resolutions on Kashmir ceased. The corpus is supplemented with OHCHR reports (2018, 2019) and Secretary-General statements to trace the post-Cold War and post-9/11 linguistic shifts.

Vocabulary Tracking

The analytical core of this project is tracking the fate of specific words across the chronological corpus. The Voyant Trends tool plots their frequency across documents — making the linguistic shift visible as a graph.

Key Finding

"The word 'plebiscite' appears 17 times in resolutions between 1948 and 1957. It appears zero times after 1971. This is not a natural evolution of language. It is the erasure of a promise."

The two word clouds make the argument visually undeniable. The early corpus is dominated by: plebiscite, people, free, impartial, vote, determination, withdrawal, demilitarisation. The late corpus is dominated by: ceasefire, parties, bilateral, stability, peace, security, terrorism. These are two completely different vocabularies for the same territory and the same people.
