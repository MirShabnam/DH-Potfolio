Epistemic Power · Network Analysis

Who Gets to Define
the Global?

A Citation Power Map of Global Studies Scholarship

A Gephi network visualisation revealing that the field of Global Studies — which claims to study the entire world — concentrates its foundational citations overwhelmingly in European and North American institutional voices. This is not incidental. It is a power structure.

Research Question

If you trace the citation networks of the most assigned global studies texts — the books that define what globalisation means, who studies it, and which frameworks are considered foundational — whose voices dominate? Which regions produce the scholars who are cited as authoritative? And whose scholarship, however rigorous, remains on the margins?

This project answers those questions with data. The result is a network map that makes epistemic power visible as a visual argument: the supposedly global field clusters almost entirely in blue.

Methods

Dataset Construction
Manual compilation of 20+ scholars, institutional affiliations, citation counts, and 40+ citation relationships from foundational Global Studies texts.
Gephi Network Map
ForceAtlas2 layout. Node size = citation count. Node colour = institutional region (Global North / Global South). Edge weight = citation relationship strength.
Interpretive Analysis
800-word scholarly essay analysing what the network reveals about the structural conditions of epistemic power in global scholarship.

Key Finding

"The network map reveals what the syllabi conceal: global studies is not a map of the globe. It is a map of where the globe has been studied from. The field that claims to transcend parochialism has its own, very specific, very located parochialism."

The visualisation shows two distinct clusters separated by a near-absence of edges between them. The Global North cluster is dense, heavily interconnected, and dominated by scholars at a handful of elite UK and US institutions. The Global South cluster is sparse, weakly connected, and largely connected to the main cluster only through a small number of bridge scholars — Appadurai, Spivak — who are based at Global North institutions.
