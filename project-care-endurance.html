Care vs
Endurance

Han Kang & László Krasznahorkai — A Digital Humanities Comparative Study

A data-driven comparison of two Nobel laureates from radically different literary traditions — Han Kang's Korea and László Krasznahorkai's Hungary — using TEI encoding, sentiment analysis, and network visualisation to examine divergent philosophical responses to historical trauma and the collapse of meaning.

Research Question

How do two writers from radically different postcolonial and post-Communist literary contexts construct philosophically distinct responses to historical violence? And what can computational analysis reveal about their literary philosophies that close reading alone cannot?

This project argues that Han Kang and Krasznahorkai represent two distinct cultural epistemologies for surviving the 20th century — Care and Endurance — and that the formal and lexical differences between their prose are the philosophical argument, not merely its surface.

Methods

TEI-XML Corpus
Structured TEI encoding of primary text excerpts from both authors.
Sentiment Analysis
VADER and TextBlob sentiment scoring across both corpora — mapping emotional register.
Theme Lexicon
Custom CSV lexicon tracking Care and Endurance theme families across both corpora.
Network Visualisation
Co-occurrence network mapping semantic relationships and thematic clusters.

Key Findings

"Han Kang's corpus: mean sentiment –0.18. Krasznahorkai's corpus: mean sentiment –0.31. Han Kang's key terms cluster around body, wound, witness. Krasznahorkai's around waiting, ruin, silence. These are not merely stylistic differences. They are epistemological ones."

Han Kang's philosophical response to violence is Care: the insistence on remaining present to suffering, on bearing witness, on refusing the numbing distance that would make trauma bearable. Krasznahorkai's is Endurance: a metaphysical stoicism that acknowledges the world's fundamental tendency toward ruin and persists anyway, without redemption or consolation.
