Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, and Alexander — A Digital Humanities Mapping

A digital humanities mapping of how Greek philosophical wisdom — rooted in Socrates's moral inquiry into how one should live — transformed, through Plato's theory of rational governance and Aristotle's natural hierarchy, into the intellectual infrastructure of Alexander's empire.

Argument

Socrates asked: how should one live? Plato answered: according to reason and the Good — which only philosophers fully grasp, making them the natural rulers of the city. Aristotle systematised this into a hierarchy of rational beings, with Greeks at its apex, and provided Alexander with a tutor and an ideology. Alexander deployed that hierarchy as a justification for the universal claims of empire.

The line from Socratic inquiry to Macedonian conquest is not a betrayal of philosophy. It is philosophy's dangerous universalist logic — the claim that reason reveals a hierarchy — taken to its political conclusion.

Key Finding

"The most dangerous ideas are not the obviously violent ones. They are the universalist ones — the ideas that claim to speak for all of reason, all of humanity, the Form of the Good itself. It is universalism, not particularism, that justifies empire."
