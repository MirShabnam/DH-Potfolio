Epistemology · Environmental Representation

Nature: Poetry
or Report?

The Cultural Politics of Environmental Representation

A computational text analysis comparing pre-industrial nature poetry with contemporary scientific environmental reports — reading the shift from literary-poetic to documentary-scientific modes as a symptom of deeper changes in cultural authority, knowledge power, and the epistemology of the natural world.

Research Question

How did humanity's authoritative representation of the natural world change from the literary-poetic to the scientific-documentary — and what does that shift reveal about which institutions get to speak with authority about nature, and on what terms?

This project argues that the move from Wordsworth's "spots of time" to the IPCC's carbon budget projections is not simply a generic shift. It is an epistemological transformation: from knowledge-as-relation to knowledge-as-measurement, from nature as inhabited to nature as data.

Methods

TEI-XML Corpus
Structured encoding of poetic and report corpora using Text Encoding Initiative standards.
Python · NLTK
Token frequency, type-token ratio, sentiment analysis (VADER), and lexical diversity metrics.
Co-occurrence Networks
NetworkX visualisation of semantic relationships across both corpora.
Comparative Analysis
Statistical comparison of sentiment, lexical density, and keyword distribution between poetic and scientific corpora.

Corpus

Poetic corpus: Wordsworth, Keats, John Clare, Tagore, Faiz Ahmad Faiz — pre-industrial and early industrial nature poetry from European and South Asian traditions.

Report corpus: IPCC Assessment Reports, NASA climate data summaries, UNEP environmental reports — contemporary scientific-documentary texts about the natural world.

Key Findings

"Poetic corpus mean sentiment: +0.34. Report corpus mean sentiment: –0.12. Poetic type-token ratio: 0.71. Report type-token ratio: 0.43. These are not just stylistic differences. They are the signatures of two different relationships to the natural world."

The keyword analysis is the most striking finding. The poetic corpus clusters around: green, still, sweet, love, light, heart, deep, living, wild, free. The report corpus clusters around: increase, global, temperature, species, loss, emissions, risk, decline, extreme, threat. These two vocabularies share almost no words. The co-occurrence network visualises this as two semantic worlds with near-zero overlap.
